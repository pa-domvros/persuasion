THE LANGUAGE OF PERSUASION

Persuasion is the deliberate use of language to shape attitudes and motivate action.

Devised by Panagiotis Domvros, English & Drama Teacher, 2025

What is persuasion? Overview

Persuasion often combines several appeals: to logic, to emotion, to authority, and to ethos (credibility/character). Effective writers select and blend appeals based on their audience and purpose.

Note: Appeals frequently work together. A strong argument (logos) can be supported by credible sources (authority, ethos) and vivid storytelling (pathos).
Core appeals
LOGOS • PATHOS • AUTHORITY • ETHOS
  • Appeal to logic (Logos) — arguments and evidence.
  • Appeal to emotion (Pathos) — feelings and values.
  • Appeal to authority — citing experts, sayings, proverbs.
  • Appeal to ethos — credibility of the speaker or of the audience.

Appeal to Logic (Logos) Reasoned proof

How it works

Leads the audience to reasonable conclusions through structured arguments and vetted sources.

Means of persuasion
  • Arguments: syllogisms, declarative claims, rhetorical questions.
  • Evidence: proofs, examples, statistics, research findings, empirical truths, quotations, testimonies, facts.

Appeal to Emotion (Pathos) Emotional engagement

How it works

Sensitizes the audience so they worry, reflect, and act on the issue at hand.

Means of persuasion
  • Description (imagery) and Narration.
  • Emotional/figurative language: charged words, irony, sarcasm, humor, hyperbole, metaphor, wordplay, ambiguity, association.
  • Rhetorical questions.

Appeal to Authority Citing expertise & wisdom

How it works

Supports the reliability of a claim by invoking voices of recognized experts or the wisdom of culture.

Means of persuasion
  • Quoting a respected personality or expert.
  • Using aphorisms, maxims, sayings, proverbs.

Appeal to Speaker’s Ethos Credibility & character

Means of persuasion
  • Trustworthy tone and consistent reasoning.
  • Praise and commendations.
  • Implicit positive characterization (projecting a responsible role model).

Appeal to Audience’s Ethos Responsibility & values

Means of persuasion
  • Direct or implicit positive characterization of the audience to encourage responsible action.

Attack on Opponent’s Ethos Undermining credibility

Means of persuasion
  • Critiques and accusations that challenge the opponent’s credibility.

Glossary Core concepts

Argument

An organized set of statements (premises) from which a conclusion follows. One premise can be phrased as a rhetorical question.

Syllogism

The method by which an argument is constructed; typically includes a major premise, a minor premise, and a conclusion.

Premises & Conclusion

Premises: statements/assumptions. Conclusion: the claim the premises lead to—the logical outcome.

Examples Logos • Pathos • Authority • Ethos

Appeal to Logic — Definition & evidence

“Recycling means processing used materials so they re-enter production, reducing raw-material consumption.”

Appeal to Logic — School context

“In schools that adopted composting, playground waste volume dropped noticeably.”

Appeal to Logic — Public policy

“Tree shade and canopies reduce urban heat islands and measurably improve microclimates.”

Appeal to Emotion — Heritage

“Picture a lit Acropolis and a child asking what ‘culture’ means—parents point to their city.”

Appeal to Emotion — Environment

“On the islands, every drop of water is a promise of continuity—let’s protect that promise.”

Appeal to Emotion — Memory

“Honoring Marathon isn’t only about a battle; it’s about the belief that together we can overcome fear.”

Appeal to Authority — Experts

“According to Prof. X, rhetorical exercises strengthen critical literacy.”

Appeal to Authority — Sayings

“Moderation in all things” — using proverbial wisdom to bolster credibility.

Speaker’s Ethos — Public address

“Respecting objections, we present our proposal and sources so citizens can decide knowledgeably.”

Audience’s Ethos — Responsibility

“A responsible citizen would agree that transparency is a prerequisite for trust.”

Attack on Opponent’s Ethos

“How can we trust proposals from someone who routinely withholds data?”

Browse the examples. In real writing, appeals are often blended creatively.
