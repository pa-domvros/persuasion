Persuasion is the deliberate use of language to shape attitudes and motivate action.

What is persuasion? Overview

Persuasion often combines several appeals: to logic, to emotion, to authority, and to ethos (credibility/character). Effective writers select and blend appeals based on their audience and purpose.

Note: Appeals frequently work together. A strong argument (logos) can be supported by credible sources (authority, ethos) and vivid storytelling (pathos).

Appeal to Logic (Logos) Reasoned proof

How it works

Leads the audience to reasonable conclusions through structured arguments and vetted sources.

Means of persuasion
  • Arguments: syllogisms, declarative claims, rhetorical questions.
  • Evidence: proofs, examples, statistics, research findings, empirical truths, quotations, testimonies, facts.

Appeal to Emotion (Pathos) Emotional engagement

How it works

Sensitizes the audience so they worry, reflect, and act on the issue at hand.

Means of persuasion
  • Description (imagery) and Narration.
  • Emotional/figurative language: charged words, irony, sarcasm, humor, hyperbole, metaphor, wordplay, ambiguity, association.
  • Rhetorical questions.

Appeal to Authority Citing expertise & wisdom

How it works

Supports the reliability of a claim by invoking voices of recognized experts or the wisdom of culture.

Means of persuasion
  • Quoting a respected personality or expert.
  • Using aphorisms, maxims, sayings, proverbs.

Glossary Core concepts

Argument

An organized set of statements (premises) from which a conclusion follows. One premise can be phrased as a rhetorical question.

Syllogism

The method by which an argument is constructed; typically includes a major premise, a minor premise, and a conclusion.

Premises & Conclusion

Premises: statements/assumptions. Conclusion: the claim the premises lead to‚Äîthe logical outcome.
